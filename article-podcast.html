Podcast Recap Social Linguistics

Our Podcast: Social Linguistics & Directed Writing

In our latest podcast episode, we explored two fascinating topics that are essential for English learners and language enthusiasts alike: social linguistics and directed writing.

What is Social Linguistics?

Social linguistics is the study of how language varies based on social factors like age, gender, class, and cultural background. Dr. Hale explained that language isn't just a tool for communication—it's also a marker of identity and belonging.

"Every time we speak, we're not just sharing information—we're signaling who we are and which groups we identify with. The way you say 'hello' to a friend vs. a colleague, or the slang you use with peers vs. formal language in a job interview—these are all examples of social linguistics in action."

— Dr. Marcus Hale, Stanford University

We discussed real-world examples, such as how workplace communication differs across cultures. For instance, direct communication is valued in Western workplaces, while indirect communication (to avoid conflict) is more common in Eastern cultures. Understanding these differences can help avoid misunderstandings in global teams.

Directed Writing: Tips from the Podcast

The second half of the podcast focused on directed writing—structured writing tasks that require you to follow specific guidelines (e.g., writing a letter, report, or speech). Dr. Hale shared practical tips to excel at these tasks, which are common in exams like IELTS, TOEFL, and academic assessments.

Key Tips for Directed Writing

  • Analyze the task first: Identify the purpose (e.g., to persuade, inform, or request), target audience, and required format (e.g., formal letter, email).
  • Plan before writing: Spend 5-10 minutes outlining your main points—this ensures your writing stays focused and logical.
  • Match tone to purpose: Use formal language for academic reports or business letters, and casual language only if the task specifies (e.g., a letter to a friend).
  • Include all required elements: If the task asks for three specific points, make sure you address all of them—missing elements lead to lower scores.
  • Edit for clarity: After writing, check for grammar errors, awkward sentences, and whether your message is clear to the target audience.

We also worked through a sample task: writing a formal email to a university professor requesting an extension on an assignment. We demonstrated how to structure the email, use appropriate tone, and clearly state the request with a valid reason—without being overly casual or demanding. We hope these stuff are useful for those G2 students.
